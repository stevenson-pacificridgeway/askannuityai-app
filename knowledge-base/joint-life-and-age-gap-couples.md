# Joint-Life Income and Age-Gap Couples
### Worked educational example: a 60-year-old and a 70-year-old considering $500,000 for lifetime income

This is a common and thoughtful question. Here's how an educator would frame it — *illustratively*, since real numbers depend on the specific contract, carrier, state, and date.

## Why the 10-year age gap matters
With a younger spouse (60) and an older spouse (70), a **joint-life** structure is usually the heart of the conversation, because the income should continue until the **second** of them passes — potentially a **35+ year** horizon driven by the younger spouse's longevity. That long expected payout period is exactly why joint-life payouts are **lower** than single-life: the insurer is guaranteeing across two lives, the younger of whom may live for decades.

## Two ways to turn $500,000 into joint lifetime income

**Path A — Immediate income (joint SPIA).** Income starts now for both lives. Joint life reduces the payment by roughly **12%–16%** versus a single life. As a rough, illustrative anchor: a life-only SPIA near these ages runs in the low-$600s/month per $100k, so **$500,000 joint-life** might generate on the order of **~$2,400–$2,800/month** (~$29,000–$34,000/year) for as long as **either** spouse lives. Simple and certain, but generally **irreversible and illiquid**.

**Path B — Fixed indexed annuity with a joint income rider.** The **$500,000 becomes the benefit base.** If they turn income on now, the **joint payout percentage is set by the younger spouse's age (60)** — roughly **3.5%–4.0%** joint — producing about **$17,500–$20,000/year for life**, while the **account value stays protected from market loss** and any remainder can pass to heirs. If instead they **wait**, the benefit base may **roll up (often ~5%–7%/year)**, and the payout percentage rises with the younger spouse's age — so deferring a few years can meaningfully increase the lifetime paycheck.

## "Would it be wise?" — the educational answer
There's no universal yes or no — it depends on their full picture. It tends to make **more** sense when:
- They have an **income gap** between essential expenses and Social Security/pensions;
- They are **loss-averse** and value a paycheck that can't be outlived;
- They want to **protect the surviving (likely younger) spouse** for a long life;
- They can commit a **portion** (rarely all) of savings and keep other money liquid.

It tends to make **less** sense when essentials are already covered by guaranteed sources, when they need full liquidity, or when one spouse has a short life expectancy.

## Key decisions for this couple
- **Joint vs. single life** (joint protects the survivor; pays a bit less).
- **Income now vs. deferring** (deferring can grow the benefit base and raise the payout %).
- **How much of the $500,000** to commit vs. keep liquid.
- **Tax location** (IRA vs. non-IRA money changes the tax treatment of the income).
- **Surrender schedule** and the **rider fee** (~0.95%–1.25%/yr).

The right move is the one that covers their **essential income for both lives** while leaving enough flexibility for everything else — then verifying every figure on a real, current illustration with a licensed professional.
