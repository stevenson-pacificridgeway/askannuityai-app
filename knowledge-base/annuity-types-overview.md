# Annuity Types: A Plain-English Overview

An annuity is a contract with an insurance company. You give the company money — all at once or over time — and in return it makes you a set of promises about safety, growth, or income. The five types people most often meet:

## 1. Multi-Year Guaranteed Annuity (MYGA)
The insurance industry's version of a bank CD. You deposit a lump sum and the insurer **locks in a fixed interest rate for a set term** (commonly 3, 5, 7, or 10 years) with **zero exposure to market losses**. In 2026, top MYGA rates run roughly **5.0%–5.75%**, and the best 5-year MYGAs reach about **6.3%** — typically **1–2 percentage points higher than a comparable CD**. Growth is **tax-deferred** (you don't pay tax until you withdraw), unlike a CD whose interest is taxed yearly. Backed by the insurer plus state guaranty associations rather than the FDIC. Best for safe, predictable growth on money you won't need for the term.

## 2. Fixed Indexed Annuity (FIA)
Principal protection **plus** the chance for more growth than a MYGA. Your money is **not invested in the market**. Instead, the insurer credits interest based on the movement of a market index (like the S&P 500), **up to a limit**, in good years — and credits **zero (never a loss)** in down years. The trade for that protection is a ceiling on the upside (see "Index Crediting"). FIAs can also add an **income rider** to guarantee lifetime income (see "Income Riders"). Best for money you can't afford to lose but still want to grow.

## 3. Single Premium Immediate Annuity (SPIA)
You hand the insurer a lump sum and **income starts almost immediately** — usually within a year — and continues for life (or a set period). The simplest way to turn savings into a paycheck. Best for someone who needs income now and values maximum certainty over flexibility.

## 4. Deferred Income Annuity (DIA)
Like a SPIA, but you **start income later** (a future date you choose). Because the insurer holds the money longer, the eventual payout is higher. Sometimes called "longevity insurance." Best for guaranteeing income that kicks in years down the road.

## 5. Variable Annuity (VA)
Your money **is** invested in market sub-accounts, so it can grow more — but it can also **lose value**, and fees are typically higher. Often paired with optional guarantees at extra cost. Best for those comfortable with market risk inside a tax-deferred wrapper. (Note: an FIA is **not** a variable annuity — the FIA protects principal; the VA does not.)

## How to choose
Match the **tool to the job**: MYGA for safe growth, FIA for protected growth (and optional income), SPIA/DIA for guaranteed income now or later, VA for market growth with risk. Most retirees use a blend — the "invest the assets, buy the income" approach — guaranteeing the income they need with one slice and keeping the rest flexible.
