# How Income Riders (GLWB) Work — and the Math

A **Guaranteed Lifetime Withdrawal Benefit (GLWB)**, often just called an **income rider**, is an optional feature you add to a fixed indexed annuity (or some variable annuities). It guarantees a paycheck **for life** — even if the underlying account value eventually runs to zero. This is the single most important concept for retirement income, so it's worth getting the vocabulary exactly right.

## Two different "values" — never confuse them
- **Account value (accumulation value):** the *real, spendable* money in your annuity. It grows with index credits, it can be surrendered, and whatever is left passes to your heirs.
- **Benefit base (income value / income base):** a **separate accounting figure used only to calculate your guaranteed income.** It is **not** a pile of cash you can withdraw in a lump sum. Confusing these two is the most common annuity mistake.

## The roll-up: how the benefit base grows before income starts
While you wait (the "deferral" period), many riders **grow the benefit base at a guaranteed roll-up rate**, commonly **5%–8% per year** (compound roll-ups in the **6%–7%** range are common on competitive contracts) — *even in years the market is flat or down.* Remember: this growth applies to the **benefit base used for income**, not to the spendable account value.

## The payout: how income is calculated
When you turn income on, the insurer multiplies your **benefit base × a payout percentage** set by your **age at activation**. A representative single-life schedule (varies by carrier):

| Age at activation | Single-life payout % |
|---|---|
| 60 | ~4.0% |
| 65 | ~5.0% |
| 70 | ~5.5% |
| 75 | ~6.0% |
| 80 | ~7.0% |

**Example:** a **$400,000 benefit base** at 5.0% = **$20,000/year for life.**

## Joint life (covering both spouses)
If you choose **joint life**, income continues until the **second** spouse passes — so the payout percentage is **about 0.5% lower** than single life (the insurer is paying across two lifetimes). On that same $400,000 benefit base: **5.5% single-life = $22,000/year**, versus **5.0% joint-life = $20,000/year.** For a married couple relying on the income, the slightly lower rate usually buys priceless peace of mind.

## The cost
Income riders aren't free. The annual **rider fee** in 2026 most commonly runs **0.95%–1.25%** of the benefit base (some up to 1.50%), deducted from the **account value** each year. That fee buys the lifetime guarantee and the roll-up — but it's a real cost to weigh.

## Why it defuses the biggest retirement risks
Because the income is a **contractual guarantee, not a withdrawal from investments**, it keeps paying through market crashes (sequence-of-returns risk) and for as long as you live (longevity risk) — the two dangers that wreck do-it-yourself withdrawal plans.
