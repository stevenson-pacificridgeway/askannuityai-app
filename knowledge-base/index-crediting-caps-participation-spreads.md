# Index Crediting Explained: Caps, Participation Rates, and Spreads

A fixed indexed annuity's defining feature is that your money is **not in the market**. The insurer simply **uses an index (like the S&P 500) as a measuring stick** to decide how much interest to credit. In up years you get a share of the gain; in down years you get **zero, not a loss**. Three "levers" control how much of the index's gain you receive:

## 1. Cap rate
The **maximum** interest you can be credited in a period. If your cap is **7%** and the index rises **10%**, you're credited **7%**. If the index rises **5%**, you're credited **5%** (under the cap). For 2026, annual point-to-point **S&P 500 caps from top carriers commonly range about 8%–14%**, with some alternative-index strategies higher.

## 2. Participation rate
The **percentage of the index's gain** you receive. At an **80% participation rate**, a **10%** index gain credits **8%**. Custom/uncapped index strategies often use participation rates from **80% to 130%+** (sometimes with no cap, but on lower-volatility indices).

## 3. Spread (a.k.a. margin or asset fee)
A flat percentage **subtracted** from the index gain before crediting. With a **2% spread**, a **10%** index gain credits **8%**. Spreads hit small gains hardest — a **2% spread on a 3% gain** wipes out two-thirds of the return.

## Common crediting methods
- **Annual point-to-point with a cap** — compares the index start vs. end of the year; simplest and most popular.
- **Participation rate** — credits a percentage of the move.
- **Performance-triggered** — credits a fixed rate if the index is flat or up.
- **Monthly point-to-point / monthly sum** — adds up monthly moves (often with a **monthly cap of 1%–3%**); can shine in steady markets but suffer if gains spike in a few months.
- **Annual monthly averaging** — averages the index across the year.

## The fine print that matters
Caps, participation rates, and spreads **reset every contract anniversary** and can change — but contracts include **guaranteed minimums** (for example, a floor cap or minimum participation rate) so the insurer can't set them to nothing. Always ask for the **current and the guaranteed-minimum** values, and which **index and method** you're choosing.

## The honest summary
You trade **some upside** for **no downside**. For money whose job is protected growth, that trade is the entire point. For money meant to maximize growth, it's the wrong tool.
